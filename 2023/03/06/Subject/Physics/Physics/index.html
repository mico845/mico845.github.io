Treating an object as a mass point is conditional and relative. So, we must make specific analysis for specific cases.

2 Motion in a Circle

when motion is in a circle,
angular velocity

\begin{align*} w &= \lim \limits_{\Delta t \to 0}{\frac{\Delta \theta}{\Delta t}} = \frac{d \theta}{dt} \end{align*}

unit: rad⋅s−1rad \cdot s^{-1}rad⋅s−1

speed

\begin{align*} v &= \lim \limits_{\Delta t \to }{\frac{\Delta s}{\Delta t}}=r \lim \limits_{\Delta t \to 0}{\frac{\Delta \theta}{\Delta t}}\\ v(t) &= r w(t) \end{align*}

velocity

\begin{align*} \overrightarrow{v} &= \frac{ds}{dt}~\overrightarrow{e_{t}}\\ &= v~\overrightarrow{e_{t}}\\ &= rw ~\overrightarrow{e_{t}} \end{align*}

2.1 Tangential and normal acceleration

\begin{align*} \overrightarrow{a} &= \frac{d \overrightarrow{v}}{dt}= \frac{d (v ~\overrightarrow{e_{t}})}{dt} = \frac{dv}{dt} ~ \overrightarrow{e_{t}} + v \frac{d~\overrightarrow{e_{t}}}{dt} \end{align*}

then we discuss these two parts separately
tangent direction:

\begin{align*} a_{t} &= \frac{dv}{dt}=\frac{d(wr)}{dt} = r\frac{dw}{dt} \end{align*}

let α=dwdt\alpha = \frac{dw}{dt}α=dtdw​, call it tangential acceleration
unit: rad⋅s2rad \cdot s^{2}rad⋅s2
we also obtain

\begin{align*} a_{t} &= r \alpha \end{align*}

normal direction:

\begin{align*} \lim \limits_{\Delta t \to 0}{\frac{\Delta \overrightarrow{e_{t}}}{\Delta t}} &= \frac{d~\overrightarrow{e_{t}}}{dt} = \frac{d\theta}{dt} ~\overrightarrow{e_{n}} \end{align*}

we also obtain

\begin{align*} \overrightarrow{a_{n}} &= v \cdot w ~\overrightarrow{e_{n}} = w^{2}r~\overrightarrow{e_{n}}=\frac{v^{2}}{r}~\overrightarrow{e_{n}} \end{align*}

when motion is not in a circle,
curvature

\begin{align*} \Delta s &=\mathop{M_{0}M'}\limits^{\frown} -\mathop{M_{0}M}\limits^{\frown}=\mathop{MM'}\limits^{\frown} \end{align*}

then

\begin{align*} \left( \frac{\Delta s}{\Delta x} \right)^{2} &=\left( \frac{\mathop{MM'}\limits^{\frown}}{\Delta x} \right)^{2}\\ & = \left( \frac{\mathop{MM'}\limits^{\frown}}{|MM'|} \right)^{2} \cdot \left( \frac{|MM'|}{\Delta x} \right)^{2}\\ & = \left( \frac{\mathop{MM'}\limits^{\frown}}{|MM'|} \right)^{2} \cdot \frac{(\Delta x)^{2}+(\Delta y)^{2}}{(\Delta x)^{2}}\\ & = \left( \frac{\mathop{MM'}\limits^{\frown}}{|MM'|} \right)^{2} \cdot \left[ 1 + \left( \frac{\Delta y}{\Delta x} \right)^{2} \right] \end{align*}

then

\begin{align*} \frac{\Delta s}{\Delta x} &= \pm \sqrt{\left( \frac{\mathop{MM'}\limits^{\frown}}{|MM'|} \right)^{2} \cdot \left[ 1 + \left( \frac{\Delta y}{\Delta x} \right)^{2} \right]}\\ \lim \limits_{\Delta x \to 0 }{\frac{\Delta s}{\Delta x}} &= \pm \sqrt{1 \cdot \left[ 1 + \left( \frac{dy}{dx}\right)^{2} \right]}\\ \frac{ds}{dx} &= \pm \sqrt{1 + y^{'2}} \end{align*}

Since Δs>=0\Delta s >=0Δs>=0, we obtain

\begin{align*} ds &= \sqrt{1 + y^{'2}} ~dx \end{align*}


Let

\begin{align*} \overline{K}=|\frac{\Delta \alpha}{\Delta s}|,~K =\lim \limits_{\Delta t \to 0}{|\frac{\Delta \alpha}{\Delta s}|} \end{align*}

call them average curvature and instantaneous curvature.

When K=0, Δα=0K = 0,~\Delta \alpha = 0K=0, Δα=0,

then

\begin{align*} K &= \frac{1}{a} \end{align*}

let’s calculator it

then we obtain,

\begin{align*} y' &= \tan \alpha\\ \alpha &= \arctan\frac{dy}{dx} = y'\\ \frac{d\alpha}{dx}&= \frac{d\left( \arctan \left( \frac{dy}{dx} \right) \right)}{dx}= \frac{y''}{1+\left( \frac{dy}{dx} \right)^{2}}\\ &= \frac{y''}{1+y^{'2}} \end{align*}

All in all,

\begin{align*} \Delta \overrightarrow{a} &= \Delta \overrightarrow{a_{t}} + \Delta \overrightarrow{a_{n}}\\ &= \frac{dv}{dt}~\overrightarrow{e_{t}} + \frac{v^{2}}{\rho}~\overrightarrow{e_{n}}\\ &= r \alpha ~\overrightarrow{e_{t}} + \frac{v^{2}}{\rho}~\overrightarrow{e_{n}} \end{align*}

2.3 Circular motion with constant speed and with constant change rate of speed

2.3.1 With constant speed

if vvv is a constant, w=vrw = \frac{v}{r}w=rv​ is also constant.

\begin{align*} a_{t} &= 0,~a_{n}=rw^{2}=\frac{v^{2}}{r}\\ \overrightarrow{a} &= a_{n}~\overrightarrow{e_{n}} = rw^{2}~\overrightarrow{e_{n}} \end{align*}

\begin{align*} w &= \frac{d \theta}{dt}\\ d \theta &= wdt\\ \int_{\theta_{0}}^{\theta}d\theta &= \int_{0}^{t}wdt\\ \theta - \theta_{0} &= w(t - 0)\\ \theta &= \theta_{0} + wt \end{align*}

2.3.2 With constant change rate of speed

if α\alphaα is a constant, then

\begin{align*} a_{t} &= r \alpha,~a_{n} = r w^{2}\\ \alpha &= \frac{dw}{dt}\\ dw &= dt \alpha\\ \int^{w}_{w_{0}} dw &= \int^{t}_{0}dt \alpha\\ w - w_{0} &= \alpha t\\ w &= w_{0} + \alpha t\\ \frac{d \theta}{dt} &= w_{0} + \alpha t\\ d \theta &= w_{0} d t + \alpha t d t\\ \int^{\theta}_{\theta_{0}}d \theta &= \int^{t}_{0}w_{0} d t + \int^{t}_{0}\alpha t d t\\ \theta - \theta_{0} &= w_{0}t+\frac{1}{2} \alpha t^{2}\\ \theta &= \theta_{0} + w_{0}t+\frac{1}{2} \alpha t^{2} \end{align*}

AND

\begin{align*} \alpha &= \frac{dw}{dt} \cdot \frac{d \theta}{d \theta}\\ \alpha &= w\frac{dw}{d \theta}\\ \alpha d \theta &= w dw\\ \int^{\theta}_{\theta_{0}}\alpha d \theta &= \int^{w}_{w_{0}} w dw\\ \alpha(\theta - \theta_{0}) &= \frac{1}{2}(w^{2} - w_{0}^{2})\\ w^{2} &= w_{0}^{2}+2\alpha(\theta - \theta_{0}) \end{align*}

\begin{align*} \begin{cases} w = w_{0} + \alpha t\\ \theta = \theta_{0} + w_{0}t+\frac{1}{2} \alpha t^{2}\\ w^{2} = w_{0}^{2}+2\alpha(\theta - \theta_{0}) \end{cases} \end{align*}

3 Relative Motion

3.1 Time and space

In the two reference frames moving relative to each other, the measurement of the time and space is absolute, regardless of the reference frame.
The absoluteness of the time and length is the foundation of classical or Newton’s mechanics.
